## Anti-Patterns Verdict

**LLM assessment**: The interface is clean and functionally rational but not distinctive. The color token system is principled (mechanics-specific encodings, Atkinson Hyperlegible, warm paper tones) — these are non-default choices that serve the audience. However, the `.label` class — `0.75rem 700 letter-spacing:0.07em text-transform:uppercase` — appears as an eyebrow above every distinct content block: "Learning Objective," "What You Are Seeing," "Quick Check," "Real-World Example," "Concept Insight," "Resolution Equations." Six tracked-uppercase eyebrows in a 320px panel is the absolute-ban pattern (tiny uppercase tracked eyebrow above every section). The `.insight-callout` and `.realworld-callout` both use a `border-left: 3px solid` accent stripe — both appear simultaneously in the same scrollable panel, which is the side-stripe border ban applied to two consecutive elements. Nothing here reads as egregiously AI-generated, but the two pattern violations and the uniform content treatment produce a panel interior that feels scaffolded rather than designed.

## Overall Impression

Technically careful, educationally disciplined, visually undifferentiated. The step copy is the strongest asset — genuinely clear plain language for a student who is scared of mechanics. The concept-check interaction is the emotional high point of the experience. Everything else is a 320px column of stacked callout boxes that feel like a template was applied rather than a teaching experience was designed. The single biggest opportunity: trust the canvas. Right now the diagram is a display surface and the text panel is the teacher. The simulation's real power is the live interactive diagram — and it is not doing enough of the teaching.

## What's Working

**1. The step copy earns its place.** "Part of the force acts horizontally — pushing left or right. This horizontal portion is called the X-component, written as Fx." Plain language first, notation second, one concept per sentence. Steps 1–3 each govern exactly one question for the student. This structural discipline is rare and valuable.

**2. The concept-check is well-designed for an anxious student.** Three options, immediate visual feedback, correct answer revealed on wrong selection, a plain-language explanation that doubles as a teaching moment. The `cc-option--correct` / `cc-option--wrong` states are complete (including the faded wrong option). For Priya the exam-anxious student, this is the moment the tool says "you understood this" — and it works.

**3. The token system is principled and mechanics-appropriate.** Force color encodings (applied red, reaction green, resultant blue-grey, moment purple), Atkinson Hyperlegible for readability, IBM Plex Mono for equations and live readouts, a warm paper background rather than clinical white. These are considered non-default choices that serve a student audience.

## Priority Issues

### [P0] Three.js load failure produces no user-facing error

**Why it matters**: If the CDN is slow, blocked by a college network firewall (extremely common in Indian institutions where the audience lives), or the browser doesn't support import maps, the canvas stays blank indefinitely. The student cannot distinguish "loading" from "broken." This silently blocks the entire simulation for a non-trivial percentage of the target audience before they see a single thing.

**Fix**: In `main.js`, add a 5-second timeout after `initScene()`. On failure, replace the empty-state copy with a visible error: "The diagram didn't load. Check your internet connection or try Chrome / Firefox." Also wrap the Three.js init in a try/catch for synchronous failures. Two-line fix with significant user impact.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable harden`

---

### [P1] Both sliders are visible from Step 1, before their purpose is established

**Why it matters**: Step 1's goal is "understand what a force vector is." The student sees Magnitude F and Angle θ simultaneously. The hint instructs them to drag the angle slider. The magnitude slider is an unexplained distractor. When a student moves the magnitude slider on Step 1, the arrow grows with no explanation of what magnitude means in component terms — that concept belongs to Step 4. This violates the progressive disclosure principle the CLAUDE.md explicitly calls for, and creates premature complexity for the weakest learners.

**Fix**: In `src/steps.js`, remove `'magnitude'` from the Step 1 controls array. Show only `'angle'` on Step 1. This is a single data change — the architecture already supports per-step control visibility.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable harden`

---

### [P1] Step 4's panel is too long and its ordering does not match the learning task

**Why it matters**: Step 4 stacks lead + what-you-see + hint + guidance + two control rows + four-row equation grid + real-world callout in a 320px column. The equations — the step's core teaching payload — sit between the sliders and the real-world callout. The student must scroll past equations to reach the Next button. On mobile (50vw canvas height, step panel below), Priya cannot see the canvas and the equations simultaneously.

**Fix**: (1) Move the equations panel to immediately follow the lead paragraph, before the controls — so the formula is visible when the student moves the sliders. (2) Remove the real-world callout from Step 4 specifically; it's more useful at Steps 2–3 where it illustrates a single-component concept. (3) On mobile, consider a sticky equation strip at the viewport bottom while the panel scrolls.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable layout`

---

### [P2] Step 5 has no completion state, no summary, and no clear exit

**Why it matters**: The last emotional beat of the simulation is the "Start over" button. For Priya preparing for an exam, arriving at "Start over" without confirmation that she learned something, a formula reference she can keep, or a pointer to the next topic (Resultant Forces) is an anti-climax. The peak-end rule says the end is what users remember. This end is flat.

**Fix**: Add a sixth card state (not a full step) that appears after pressing Next on Step 5. It should: confirm the concept was covered ("You've worked through vector resolution"), show the two take-away formulas (Fx = F cos θ, Fy = F sin θ) as a compact reference, and include a secondary "Next topic: Resultant Forces →" link. "Start over" remains as a secondary action.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable onboard`

---

### [P2] Screen reader users receive no audio feedback about canvas state on Steps 1–3

**Why it matters**: The equations panel has `aria-live="polite"` but only renders on Steps 4–5. On Steps 1–3, Sam moves the angle slider and hears the number input change but receives no information about what happened in the diagram. "The teal dashed arrow is Fx" is read once in the step text — there is no live announcement of component values before the equation panel appears. The canvas is `aria-hidden="true"` with `role="img"`, meaning it is a static label throughout the entire session.

**Fix**: In `uiManager.js`'s `updateReadouts` method, also update a visually-hidden `aria-live="polite"` region that exists on all steps, not just when the equation panel is mounted. Minimal copy: "Force 200 N at 45 degrees. Horizontal component 141 N. Vertical component 141 N." One hidden `<div>` in `index.html`, one extra update in `updateReadouts`.

**Suggested command**: `$impeccable audit`

## Persona Red Flags

**Jordan (Confused First-Timer)**

Jordan opens the simulation. The canvas is blank while Three.js loads. There is no animation, no spinner, no message — just the empty-state text "Your diagram will appear here." He reads this and does not know if the page failed to load or is still working. Three.js initializes after ~1 second and the red arrow appears without transition or announcement. Jordan sees two sliders labeled "Magnitude F" and "Angle θ" and doesn't know which to touch first. The hint instructs him to drag the angle slider, but it sits below the magnitude slider — his eye lands on magnitude first. On Step 5 he clicks "Start over" expecting a completion screen. He returns to Step 1 and does not know whether he finished the module.

**Sam (Accessibility-Dependent User)**

Sam tabs through Step 1. The step counter has `aria-live="polite"` — good. The Next/Back buttons have `aria-label` attributes — good. Sam moves the angle slider on Step 2. The numeric input value changes. No `aria-live` region announces a component value. The equation panel has `aria-live="polite"` but does not exist on Step 2. Sam has no audio indication of what happened to the force vector. The `.rail-dot` elements are 28px — below the 44px touch target minimum. The `cc-option` buttons use `outline: none` with a `:focus-visible` fallback that may be invisible in older browsers on shared college lab computers.

**Priya (Struggling B.Tech Student, 2 Days Before Exam)**

Priya opens the simulation on her phone. The canvas is 50vw high (~200px). She reads the step text below it but cannot see the diagram and the text at the same time without scrolling. On Step 4 she sees four equations appear. She does not know intuitively why cosine gives the horizontal component. The step copy says "Fx and Fy meet at exactly 90°" but does not explain the trigonometric basis. There is no concept-check on Step 4 — the moment she needs validation most, the validation mechanism is absent. She finishes Step 5, clicks "Start over," and has no formula reference to photograph for her notes.

## Minor Observations

- The `btn-secondary` (Back) uses `visibility: hidden` on Step 1 rather than `display: none`. It occupies layout space and the Next button does not expand to fill the gap. On mobile, this leaves dead space in the button row.
- The `hint-callout` and `guidance-callout` serve the same structural role (deliver contextual text below the lead) but have different colors and prefix icons (`ℹ` vs `→`). Students cannot reliably distinguish "informational" from "directed observation" from visual style alone — the distinction is not worth two separate callout types.
- The angle range 0–360° is correct, but dragging past 90° on Steps 2–4 produces negative Fx values with no explanation. The insight text that explains this only appears at Step 5. A student who sees `Fx = -87 N` on Step 2 has no context for the negative sign.
- The `realworld-callout` and `insight-callout` both use `border-left: 3px solid` in immediate proximity in the Step 4–5 panel. Two adjacent side-stripe callouts with different accent colors visually compete.
- `FORCE_SCALE` is imported into `uiManager.js` from `sceneSetup.js` and used only in `updateLabels` for world-to-screen position math. This couples the UI manager to the 3D layer's internal scaling constant — a fragile dependency if `FORCE_SCALE` ever changes.

## Questions to Consider

**1. What if the canvas did more of the teaching?**
Right now the diagram shows what's happening and the text panel explains what it means. Every educational action is in a 320px column. What if the force label on the canvas read "F = 200 N — try the slider" and the equation appeared as a canvas overlay at the moment the component arrows appeared? Would students even need five callout boxes per step?

**2. Why does the concept-check disappear at the two most important steps?**
The simulation's strongest validation moment appears exactly twice — at Steps 2 and 3 — then vanishes. Step 4 is the synthesis step (hardest concept). Step 5 is the final impression. If the concept-check is what tells a student "you understood this," why is it absent precisely at the moment the full concept lands and at the moment she closes the window?

**3. What is the minimum information required to teach each step's single concept?**
Step 4 delivers its one concept through: lead paragraph + hint + what-you-see panel + guidance callout + 2 sliders + 4-row equation grid + real-world callout. That is seven information regions for one concept. What if Step 4 were just the equations, the sliders, and one sentence?
